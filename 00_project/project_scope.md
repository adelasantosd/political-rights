## 1. Project title and subtitle

**Project title:** NormTrace-Political Rights

**Working subtitle:** Legal-institutional activation mapping of political participation rights in Mexico and Costa Rica

---

## 2. Countries

**Pilot countries:**

- Mexico (federal republic, civil law system, dualist with broad treaty incorporation via Article 1 CPEUM)
- Costa Rica (unitary republic, civil law system, monist with constitutional hierarchy of human rights)

These two countries share inter-American commitments under the American Convention on Human Rights and have ratified the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights. Their institutional designs for political participation differ in federal structure, electoral authority architecture, and the design of direct democracy mechanisms.

---

## 3. Research problem

Many legal systems formally recognise political participation rights. Constitutions include provisions on voting, candidacy, referendums, citizen initiatives, popular consultations, and revocation of mandate. Electoral codes regulate procedures. Courts interpret these provisions.

The central research problem is that formal recognition does not establish legal activation. A right or mechanism is legally activated only when the domestic legal system provides all of the following: an identified responsible authority, a defined procedure, stated activation thresholds, admissibility conditions with defined criteria, a review or remedy mechanism, a legal effect binding on the relevant authority, accessibility conditions that do not make activation unreasonably burdensome, and an implementation duty.

When one or more of these conditions is absent, incomplete, unclear or disproportionately burdensome, a legal-institutional barrier exists. The gap between formal recognition and legal activation is the object of analysis.

This project maps that gap systematically across Mexico and Costa Rica for the range of political participation rights and mechanisms within scope.

## 4. Legal systems covered

### Mexico

- Constitutional law: Constitución Política de los Estados Unidos Mexicanos (CPEUM), as amended
- Electoral law: Ley General de Instituciones y Procedimientos Electorales (LGIPE), Ley General de Partidos Políticos (LGPP), Ley Federal de Consulta Popular, Ley Federal de Revocación de Mandato, Ley General del Sistema de Medios de Impugnación en Materia Electoral (LGSMIME)
- Legislative procedure: Ley Orgánica del Congreso General, Reglamento de la Cámara de Diputados, Reglamento del Senado de la República
- Administrative regulation: INE internal regulations, lineamientos, acuerdos of the General Council
- Jurisprudence: TEPJF (Tribunal Electoral del Poder Judicial de la Federación), SCJN (Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación)
- Constitutional remedies: Ley de Amparo (where applicable to political rights)

### Costa Rica

- Constitutional law: Constitución Política de Costa Rica (1949), as amended
- Electoral law: Código Electoral (Ley 8765, 2009), Ley sobre la Regulación del Referéndum (Ley 8492), Ley de Iniciativa Popular (Ley 8491), Ley Orgánica del TSE (Ley 3504)
- Legislative procedure: Reglamento de la Asamblea Legislativa
- Administrative regulation: TSE reglamentos, acuerdos and manuals
- Jurisprudence: TSE (Tribunal Supremo de Elecciones), Sala Constitucional (Sala IV)

## 5. International and regional standards in scope

The following instruments are within analytical scope as standard layers against which domestic mechanisms are mapped:

**Binding treaties:**
- American Convention on Human Rights (ACHR / CADH), 1969, especially Article 23 (political rights) and Article 8 (fair trial, applicable to electoral remedies) and Article 25 (judicial protection)
- International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR / PIDCP), 1966, especially Article 25 (right to vote, be elected, access public service) and Article 2(3) (effective remedy)

**Authoritative interpretive materials:**
- UN Human Rights Committee, General Comment No. 25 (1996) on Article 25 ICCPR
- Inter-American Court of Human Rights (IACtHR / CorteIDH) judgments on political rights, especially: Castañeda Gutman v. Mexico (2008); Yatama v. Nicaragua (2005); López Mendoza v. Venezuela (2011); Petro Urrego v. Colombia (2020); Chitay Nech y otros v. Guatemala (2010)
- Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (IACHR / CIDH) thematic reports on political participation and democratic rights

**Declarative and soft-law instruments:**
- Inter-American Democratic Charter (OAS, 2001)
- American Declaration of the Rights and Duties of Man (1948), Article XX
- OAS Charter, provisions on representative democracy

**Comparative soft-law context (phase 2, optional):**
- Venice Commission (Council of Europe) standards on electoral law and direct democracy
- IDEA International comparative standards on direct democracy design

The legal force of each instrument is recorded in the corpus metadata and in the international standards table. Binding IACtHR judgments are distinguished from advisory opinions, commission reports, treaty body general comments, and soft-law standards.

## 6. Domestic mechanisms in scope

The following political participation mechanisms are within analytical scope:

**Electoral participation:**
- Right to vote (suffrage)
- Right to be elected (active candidacy right)
- Independent candidacies (candidaturas independientes)
- Political party participation (registration, access, internal democracy)

**Direct democracy mechanisms:**
- Referendum
- Popular consultation (consulta popular)
- Plebiscite (where legally distinct from referendum)
- Citizen legislative initiative (iniciativa ciudadana / iniciativa popular)
- Revocation of mandate (revocación de mandato)

**Other participation forms:**
- Petition right
- Public hearings (audiencias públicas) as mandated participation mechanisms
- Transparency and access to public information as enabling conditions for participation

**Remedies:**
- Electoral remedies (JDC, JRC in Mexico; TSE complaints and resources in Costa Rica)
- Constitutional review of political rights restrictions

**Phase 2 (planned):**
- Municipal and local participation mechanisms
- Subnational state-level mechanisms in Mexico
- Participatory budgeting
- Local consultations

---

## 7. Excluded areas

The following areas are outside the scope of this project:

- Assessment of overall democratic quality or democratic backsliding
- Electoral fairness as a matter of political practice or observation
- Measurement of actual use rates of mechanisms
- Analysis of partisan competition, campaign finance effectiveness, or political party strategy
- Final assessment of legal compliance with international obligations
- Programmatic or operational capacity of electoral institutions
- Comparison with countries outside the pilot scope in phase 1
- IHR, WHO, pandemic law, health governance, or any other NormTrace module

## 9. Unit of analysis

The unit of analysis is a traceable analytical chain:

**international or inter-American standard → domestic right/mechanism → legal provision → actor → activation requirement → review mechanism → legal effect → barrier type → activation level**

Each row in the core mapping table (`political_rights_mapping.csv`) represents one such chain for one mechanism in one country, traced to specific provisions and standards.

A single mechanism may produce multiple rows if it involves multiple activation requirements, multiple actors, or multiple applicable international standards.

## 11. What this project does not assess

- Whether political rights are actually exercised in practice
- Whether electoral institutions perform their functions effectively
- Whether judicial decisions are enforced
- Whether governments comply with IACtHR judgments
- Whether political parties are internally democratic in practice
- Overall democratic quality or institutional legitimacy
- Final legal compliance with international law

All outputs are preliminary. They require expert legal review before use in policy, advocacy, litigation, or legal advice contexts.
