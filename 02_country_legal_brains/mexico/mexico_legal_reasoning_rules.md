# Mexico Legal Reasoning Rules for NormTrace-IHR

---

## 1. Purpose

This file contains preliminary analytical rules for classifying the type and intensity of domestic legal anchoring of international health obligations in Mexico. These rules are designed to guide NormTrace-IHR assessments of whether a given international obligation — primarily under the International Health Regulations (IHR 2005 and its 2024 amendments) and the Pandemic Agreement — has a legally sufficient normative base within the Mexican legal order, and if so, at what level and with what degree of completeness.

The rules in this document do not assess compliance, propose legislative reforms, or substitute legal advice. They are methodological instruments for structured analytical classification. Their application produces anchoring classifications and gap typologies that feed into the NormTrace mapping tables listed in Section 8.

---

## 2. Legal Anchoring Categories

The following categories define the type of domestic normative instrument that grounds a given international health obligation within the Mexican legal system. Classification follows a hierarchy consistent with the Mexican constitutional order (Articles 89 fraction I, 90, 92, and 124 of the Political Constitution of the United Mexican States — CPEUM) and the General Health Law framework.

### A. Statutory Anchoring

An obligation has statutory anchoring when it is grounded in a formal statute (*ley en sentido formal*) — that is, a legal instrument issued by the legislative branch (Congress or a state legislature), or by the executive branch in exercise of delegated legislative authority — with sufficient legal force to create competences, obligations, restrictions, procedures, rights, sanctions, or coordination mechanisms. Statutory anchoring is required when the obligation's content exceeds what can be established through regulatory or administrative instruments alone.

In Mexico, statutory instruments include: the General Health Law (*Ley General de Salud*), the Organic Law of the Federal Public Administration (*Ley Orgánica de la Administración Pública Federal*), the National Security Law (*Ley de Seguridad Nacional*), the Federal Transparency and Access to Public Information Law, the Federal Law on Protection of Personal Data, and other federal statutes, as well as their state-level equivalents.

### B. Regulatory Anchoring

An obligation has regulatory anchoring when it can be developed through a *reglamento* (executive regulation issued under Article 89 fraction I CPEUM), an *reglamento interior*, a *disposición administrativa general*, or a technical regulation (*Norma Oficial Mexicana* — NOM, or *Norma Mexicana* — NMX), provided that a sufficient legal basis (*habilitación legal*) exists in a statute. Regulatory anchoring is appropriate where the obligation involves technical detail, procedural specification, or operational development of statutory mandates, but does not independently create new competences or rights.

NOMs issued by the Ministry of Health (*Secretaría de Salud*) under Articles 3 and 13 of the General Health Law are a primary regulatory anchoring instrument in the health domain. However, a NOM cannot substitute for statutory authorization where the content of the obligation requires it.

### C. Administrative Anchoring

An obligation has administrative anchoring when it can be implemented through an *acuerdo secretarial*, *lineamiento*, protocol, manual, operational program, or administrative practice, provided that no new competences are created, no autonomous modification of rights occurs, and the implementing instrument operates strictly within existing legal and regulatory authorizations. Administrative anchoring is inherently limited: it cannot generate binding obligations for third parties, authorize coercive measures, or establish new institutional structures.

### D. Operational Implementation Only

An obligation is classified as operational implementation only when its content is primarily technical or organizational and can be executed within existing attributed competences without any apparent need for a new normative instrument. This includes activities such as internal protocol updates, technical coordination among already-authorized actors, or logistical reorganization within existing mandates.

## 3. Anchoring Scale

Each obligation analyzed under NormTrace-IHR is assigned an anchoring score on the following scale. The score reflects the degree to which the current Mexican legal and regulatory framework provides an adequate normative foundation for domestic implementation of the obligation.

**0 — No identifiable anchoring**
No provision in the Mexican legal order — statutory, regulatory, or administrative — can be identified as providing even contextual support for the obligation. The obligation has no domestic normative base.

**1 — Indirect contextual anchoring**
General or implicit provisions exist that could be invoked as contextual support, but they do not directly address the subject matter of the obligation, do not create specific competences, and their relevance is interpretively strained. Anchoring is nominal at best.

**2 — Administrative or operational anchoring**
The obligation is addressed through administrative instruments (acuerdos, lineamientos, protocols, programs) or through operational practice, without formal statutory or regulatory development. This level is sufficient only for obligations classifiable under categories C or D above; it is insufficient for obligations requiring statutory or regulatory grounding.

**3 — Partial statutory anchoring**
A statutory or regulatory provision exists that addresses the general subject area of the obligation, but significant gaps remain: the provision does not cover the full scope of the obligation, leaves key elements undefined, lacks implementing regulations, or does not address specific operational requirements (e.g., coordination mechanisms, procedural rights, sanctions).

**4 — Strong statutory-administrative anchoring**
A statutory base and at least one implementing regulatory or administrative instrument exist and together cover the principal elements of the obligation. Remaining gaps are secondary, technical, or concerns of optimization rather than legal sufficiency.

**5 — Integrated implementation anchoring**
The obligation is comprehensively grounded in a coherent statutory-regulatory-administrative framework that addresses its principal requirements, establishes clear competences, identifies responsible actors, and provides operative mechanisms. Gaps, if present, are marginal or subject to scheduled review.

## 4. Rules Indicating Likely Need for Statutory or Regulatory Anchoring

An international health obligation likely requires strong statutory or regulatory anchoring — and cannot be adequately implemented through administrative instruments alone — when any of the following conditions apply:

- The obligation creates duties or competences for national authorities that are not already authorized by existing law.
- The obligation creates binding obligations for private parties (individuals, legal entities, laboratories, health providers, airlines, port operators, etc.).
- The obligation affects human rights, including the right to health, right to privacy, right to personal liberty, right to free movement, or due process rights.
- The obligation authorizes or requires restrictive measures, coercive actions, or the imposition of sanctions.
- The obligation requires the collection, processing, transfer, or storage of personal data or sensitive personal data (datos personales sensibles), as defined under the Federal Law on Protection of Personal Data Held by Private Parties or the Law on Protection of Personal Data Held by Obligated Subjects.
- The obligation requires mandatory information exchange — including surveillance data, genomic sequences, or epidemiological intelligence — with international organizations, foreign governments, or other national entities.
- The obligation requires formal coordination between the Federation and states or municipalities, or between federal agencies, in a manner that modifies or specifies competence boundaries.
- The obligation requires recurrent budgetary allocation or creates long-term financial commitments under Article 126 CPEUM.
- The obligation creates new institutional bodies, new functions, or new competences not currently attributed by law.
- The obligation establishes or requires enforceable procedures involving administrative or judicial oversight.
- The obligation affects travelers, international mobility, quarantine or isolation measures, points of entry (ports, airports, land border crossings), or the rights of persons subject to public health measures.
- The obligation involves laboratories, pathogens, biological specimens, biosafety requirements, export controls, or access and benefit-sharing arrangements related to genetic resources or pathogen samples.

---

## 5. Rules Indicating Possible Administrative Implementation

An international health obligation may be adequately addressed through administrative implementation — without requiring new statutory or regulatory instruments — when all of the following conditions are met:

- The obligation is limited to the internal organization, procedures, or coordination of already-authorized public bodies.
- It does not create new obligations for authorities beyond what existing legal authorizations permit.
- It does not create any obligation, restriction, or burden on private parties.
- It does not affect or restrict rights in any manner that requires legal form.
- It falls clearly within existing attributed competences and can be implemented without exceeding them.
- It is implemented through technical protocols, operational guidelines, or coordination practices that do not modify the legal status of the implementing entities.
- It develops or specifies internal procedures already authorized by statute or regulation, without expanding their scope.
- It organizes inter-institutional communication or information sharing within existing frameworks and without modifying competence allocations.

The presence of even one of the indicators in Section 4 overrides the conditions in this section: an obligation meeting any Section 4 criterion requires statutory or regulatory analysis regardless of its apparent operational simplicity.

## 6. Gap Types

The following gap typology is used in NormTrace-IHR analytical tables to characterize the nature of the normative deficit identified for a given obligation. Multiple gap types may apply simultaneously to a single obligation.

**Legal silence** — No provision in the Mexican legal order addresses the subject matter of the obligation, either directly or by reasonable implication. The gap is complete.

**Competence ambiguity** — It is unclear which authority or level of government holds the legal competence to implement the obligation, or whether the relevant competence exists at all. Multiple actors may claim or disclaim authority over the subject matter.

**Administrative-only anchoring** — The obligation is addressed exclusively through administrative instruments (programs, agreements, protocols) that lack statutory or regulatory backing. The anchor exists but is legally insufficient for the type of obligation involved.

**Procedural gap** — A substantive legal base exists, but no enforceable procedure has been established for its implementation: no defined process, timelines, notification requirements, appeals mechanisms, or judicial review pathway.

**Coordination gap** — No legal mechanism establishes how multiple actors (e.g., federal and state health authorities, IMSS, ISSSTE, CENAPRECE, InDRE, customs, immigration, agriculture, environment) are required to coordinate for implementation of the obligation.

**Federal implementation gap** — Implementation requires state or municipal action but no federal mechanism ensures that subnational entities will act consistently with the international obligation, given Mexico's federalist constitutional structure (Articles 40, 73, and 124 CPEUM).

**Rights-safeguard gap** — The obligation involves measures that affect individual rights but no provision establishes procedural guarantees, substantive limitations on state action, review mechanisms, or due process protections.

**Oversight gap** — No mechanism exists for monitoring, auditing, reporting, or ensuring accountability regarding the implementation of the obligation by responsible authorities.

**Budget/capacity gap** — No identified budgetary allocation, legal appropriation, or institutional capacity supports the implementation of the obligation. The gap may be financial, technical, infrastructural, or human-resources-related.

**Update-review needed** — A provision exists that could anchor the obligation but is outdated, predates the relevant international instrument, or has not been formally reconciled with current international standards. The gap is not one of absence but of currency.

## 7. Mexico-Specific Caution Rules

The following rules reflect structural features of the Mexican legal system that require particular analytical caution when classifying anchoring and identifying gaps. These are not interpretive choices but methodological guardrails derived from constitutional architecture and established legal doctrine.

1. **Do not infer legal sufficiency solely from the existence of a general faculty.** The presence of a broad enabling provision (e.g., Article 73 fraction XVI CPEUM granting Congress authority over health matters, or Article 3 of the General Health Law listing general health matters) does not automatically imply that a specific obligation is adequately anchored. The relevant question is whether a specific statutory or regulatory instrument has *developed* that faculty in relation to the obligation in question.

2. **Do not assume a NOM can create competences that require statutory or regulatory form.** Normas Oficiales Mexicanas are technical-normative instruments issued under the General Health Law. They can develop, specify, and operationalize existing competences. They cannot create new competences, new rights, or new obligations for parties not already subject to statutory authorization.

3. **Do not assume that an administrative agreement (*acuerdo*) is equivalent to a legislative mandate.** An *acuerdo secretarial* or *acuerdo interinstitucional* operates within existing legal parameters. It does not have the force of law, cannot bind parties outside its administrative scope, and cannot substitute for statutory or regulatory instruments where these are legally required.

4. **Do not treat administrative practice as strong normative anchoring.** Consistent institutional practice, even when formalized in internal manuals or operational protocols, does not constitute binding normative anchoring unless it is grounded in a statute, regulation, or duly published administrative instrument with binding force.

5. **Do not conflate federal health authority with state-level implementation capacity.** The *Secretaría de Salud* and its agencies (CENAPRECE, InDRE, COFEPRIS/COFEPRIS successor) hold federal competences. Health system implementation in Mexico is distributed across federally attributed functions, state-attributed functions, and concurrent functions. An obligation that requires action at ports, border points, or subnational health systems may face federal-state coordination gaps not reflected in federal law alone.

6. **Do not assume that an international obligation is self-executing without reviewing its content, subject matter, and need for domestic development.** Under Mexican constitutional doctrine, international treaties ratified by Mexico are part of the domestic legal order (Article 133 CPEUM) and rank above federal laws but below the Constitution. However, many treaty obligations — particularly those creating competences, procedures, or rights protections — require domestic development to be operative. The question of self-execution must be assessed obligation by obligation, not assumed globally.

7. **Flag for human expert review when the obligation involves any of the following:** rights affected, personal or sensitive personal data, restrictive public health measures, travelers or points of entry, laboratories, pathogens, biological specimens, genomic sequences, pathogen sample transfers, or access and benefit-sharing arrangements. These areas combine legal complexity with high human rights stakes, and classification by NormTrace-IHR should be treated as preliminary and subject to expert validation.

## 8. Use in Future NormTrace Tables

The rules and scales in this document are the analytical engine for populating the following NormTrace-IHR data files for Mexico. Each file draws on the categories, scale values, and gap types defined above.

**mexico_legal_provisions.csv** — Each row records a legal provision identified as potentially anchoring one or more IHR or Pandemic Agreement obligations. The anchoring category (Section 2), anchoring scale value (Section 3), and applicable gap types (Section 6) are determined using the rules in Sections 4, 5, and 7.

**mexico_legal_actors.csv** — Each row records a legal or institutional actor with attributed competence over one or more obligations. Competence ambiguity (Section 6) and federal implementation gaps are flagged using the caution rules in Section 7.

**mexico_ihr_mapping.csv** — Each row maps a specific IHR 2005 obligation to its identified domestic anchoring provisions, anchoring score, gap types, and human-review flags. Section 7 rule 7 governs the assignment of human-review flags.

**mexico_ihr2024_gap_projection.csv** — Each row projects the anchoring implications of IHR 2024 amendments not yet reflected in current Mexican law. Gap types from Section 6 are applied to each unanchored or partially anchored amendment.

**mexico_pandemic_agreement_mapping.csv** — Each row maps a Pandemic Agreement obligation to its domestic anchoring status using the full analytical framework of this document, including the caution rules in Section 7 as applied to benefit-sharing, data, laboratory, and pathogen-related obligations.

In all tables, anchoring scores of 0–2 should be accompanied by explicit gap type notation. Anchoring scores of 3–4 should include notation of the principal remaining gap. Human-review flags (Section 7, rule 7) should be preserved in all tables and never suppressed by automated scoring.

## 9. Limitations

The rules and criteria set out in this document are preliminary and methodological in nature. The following limitations apply to their use:

- **Preliminary status.** These rules represent an initial analytical framework. They have not been formally validated by legal experts in Mexican constitutional law, health law, or international law. They are subject to revision as the NormTrace-IHR project develops.

- **Requirement for legal validation.** Application of these rules to specific obligations must be validated by qualified legal professionals. NormTrace-IHR outputs based on these rules are analytical classifications, not legal opinions.

- **No substitution for legal advice or judicial interpretation.** Nothing in this document constitutes legal advice. Classifications produced using these rules do not substitute for judicial interpretation, administrative doctrine, or professional legal counsel. The rules do not bind any authority, court, or institution.

- **Dependence on current sources.** Rules are calibrated to the Mexican legal framework as understood at the time of drafting. They must be updated when: (a) relevant statutes are amended or new laws enacted; (b) the General Health Law, its regulations, or key NOMs are modified; (c) relevant jurisprudence of the Supreme Court of Justice of the Nation (*Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación* — SCJN) or federal courts alters interpretive parameters; (d) international instruments to which Mexico is party are amended or new obligations become applicable; or (e) constitutional amendments affect the distribution of competences in health matters.

- **Scope.** These rules address anchoring classification only. They do not cover the substance, content, or interpretation of specific obligations, the adequacy of Mexican policy responses, or the political feasibility of normative development.
